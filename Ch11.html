Key idea

Core message: Just because something is doable through technology does not make it appropriate, legal, or ethical. As analytics, cognitive computing and AI spread, everyone is affected — so the manager's job is not only to build intelligent systems but to deploy them responsibly: handle liability, protect privacy, remove bias, and manage the human impact (jobs, skills, resistance). Legal, privacy and ethical issues are interconnected — many privacy issues are part of ethics or have legal aspects.

Opening vignette — lessons from an organization's analytics journey

A checklist of what actually goes wrong in real data-science projects:

  • What kind of problem are you solving? A business problem vs a research problem — define the goal, the value, and the right business/research metrics.
  • Data sources & quality — can we even access the data inside our own organisation? Have we considered data governance?
  • Prediction vs causation/explanation — a model that predicts well does not explain why.
  • Bias–variance trade-off — bias = how far predictions are from true values; variance = how much predictions fluctuate with different training data.
  • Overfitting = the model learns the training data too well → low training error but high test error. Underfitting = the model is too simple → high error on both.
  • Maintenance of AI models — retrain when needed (models decay).
  • The right framework, in escalating order: reports & dashboards → EDA & visualization → statistical analysis & hypothesis testing → simple predictive models → deep learning → causal models.
  • Explain results in business metrics; set realistic timelines; get business buy-in frequently; mind the talent gap (finding and retaining good data scientists is hard).

Implementing intelligent systems

Once an AI system is developed, it must be put into use by the enterprise and its knowledge workers — a complex undertaking. The implementation process is similar to the generic process for other information systems, but has extra pitfalls.

Three major system-development problems (Shchutskaya & Zharovskikh, 2020)

  1. Development approach — AI development is different from ordinary IT/computer-systems development.
  2. Learning from data — the quality of the data determines the quality and usefulness of the outcomes.
  3. No clear view of how insights are generated — as the data stream changes, so may the insights (opacity + drift).

Related trouble spots: problems with Big Data, ineffective information access, limited integration capabilities.

Connectivity, integration & security

  • Connectivity & integration — the AI/analytics application must be connected to existing IT systems, the Internet, and other intelligent systems. (Example: the Australian government commissioned Microsoft to build hyperscale cloud regions.)
  • Security protection — intelligent applications managed/updated in the cloud or connected to the Internet open new vulnerabilities; robots are a further source of danger and security threat.

Leveraging intelligent systems in business

Three levers: ① customize the customer experience, ② increase customer engagement (e.g. via chatbots), ③ detect problems and anomalies in data. Critical success factors: discover, predict, justify and learn from experience; upgrade employees' skills and build an AI-savvy workforce. One of the most important issues: employees' fear of job loss.

Top management & successful deployment

Senior executives must understand the tactical and strategic opportunities of AI, redesign their organizations, and commit to shaping the future of work. Failure to respond in time is usually blamed on senior management.

Deployment-strategy questions (the manager's checklist)

  • When to embark on intelligent projects and how to prioritize them.
  • Do it yourself, partner, or outsource?
  • How to justify the investment (ROI).
  • How to overcome employee resistance (fear of job loss).
  • How to arrange appropriate people–robot teams.
  • Which decisions to fully automate by AI.
  • How to protect the systems (security) and protect privacy.
  • How to handle possible job losses and retraining.

KPMG guidelines for "digital labor": establish priority areas for technological innovation · develop a strategy and plan for employees · identify providers and partners · establish plans to realize the benefits.

Major implementation issues: organizational alignment · interoperability challenges · security · privacy · connecting the silos of data (preparing existing IT architectures and operating models for IoT is complex) · management · connected customers.

Legal issues

The recurring question is liability — and most of it is still unanswered:

  • Who is liable for the actions or advice of intelligent machines? If a firm goes bankrupt following an AI application's advice, who pays?
  • Is the enterprise responsible for not testing the system adequately before trusting it with sensitive issues? Do auditing/accounting firms share liability? Are software developers jointly liable?
  • Self-driving cars — who is liable when a car's sensors, network or AI fail?
  • What is the value of an expert opinion in court when the expertise is encoded in a computer?
  • What if a physician accepts an incorrect computer diagnosis and the patient dies? What if a manager enters an incorrect judgment value and disaster follows?
  • Can management force experts to contribute their expertise to an intelligent system — and how are they compensated?
  • Who owns the knowledge that systems like ChatGPT/Bard gather and that an end user then employs?
  • Intellectual-property protection takes on unique dimensions; Facebook has faced legal issues over face recognition; robot safety rules were written long ago. At present there are very few laws regarding intelligent technologies — most relate to safety.

AI in law (the profession itself)

AI applied to legal work: analysing legal data (e.g. regulatory conflicts) to detect patterns · providing legal advice to consumers · document review · analysing contracts · supporting legal research · predicting results (likelihood to win). Open question: ownership of content used to train AI models (e.g. the AI-art-generator lawsuits).

Privacy issues

Definition: privacy = the right to be left alone and the right to be free from unreasonable personal intrusions. Two rules from past court decisions: ① the right of privacy is NOT absolute — it must be balanced against the needs of society; ② the public's right to know is superior to the individual's right to privacy.

Collecting information about individuals

Intelligent technologies collect customer information to provide targeted services and marketing. The Internet plus large-scale databases created an entirely new dimension of access. Used for good too: reduce fraud, prevent crime, identify government mismanagement, tax evasion, welfare cheating, employment of illegal workers.

Ethical issues

Personal values are a major factor in ethical decision-making, and ethics is multidimensional — so it's complex. Crucially: some ethical issues are upsetting but not illegal.

The Facebook emotion experiment: Facebook presented different News Feeds to users and monitored their emotional reactions (replies, likes, sentiment). Many companies run similar analytics — but because Facebook is so large and it ran the experiment without users' informed consent, it was judged unethical. Facebook acknowledged the error and instituted a formal review.

The big ethical questions in AI & robotics

  • What is the impact on jobs? How do robots affect our behavior and interactions?
  • How can the wealth created by intelligent machines be distributed?
  • How do we guard against mistakes by intelligent applications?
  • Can intelligent systems be fair and unbiased — how do we eliminate bias in their creation and operation?
  • How do we keep them safe from adversaries and protected against unintended consequences (e.g. robot accidents)?
  • How do we stay in control of a complex intelligent system?

Further ethical topics: electronic surveillance · ethics in BI/AI system design · software piracy · invasion of privacy · use of proprietary databases/knowledge bases · use of personal intellectual property for company benefit (and paying contributors) · accuracy of data/information/knowledge · protection of users' rights · accessibility of information · how much decision-making to delegate to intelligent machines.

Machine ethics & roboethics

  • Machine ethics — concerned with the moral behavior of artificially intelligent beings. Roboethics — concerned with the moral behavior of the designers, builders and users of robots.
  • Cautionary tales: Microsoft's Tay chatbot was shut down after it couldn't handle offensive input; fears that algorithms may become racist; self-driving cars may one day face a decision of whom to save and whom to kill; voice technologies enable caller identification.
  • The 10 Commandments of Computer Ethics — don't use a computer to harm people, interfere with others' work, snoop in their files, steal, or bear false witness; don't copy unpaid-for software or use others' resources without authorization; don't appropriate others' intellectual output; do think about the social consequences of what you write; do use computers with consideration and respect. (Note: the last two are printed with "Thou shalt not" in the slides — that's a typo; the intent is the positive duty.)

Cautionary cases

  • BlenderBot 3.0 — Meta's chatbot going off the rails.
  • Amazon's scrapped AI recruiting tool — learned gender bias from historical hiring data and had to be abandoned.
  • COMPAS (Correctional Offender Management Profiling for Alternative Sanctions) — criminal-risk scoring accused of racial bias in sentencing decisions.

Why ethical AI should matter to managers

Netflix is the success story (DVD sales → rentals → streaming → production), and its recommender systems are built on AI + user behavioral data — yet they are not immune to bias. The stakes are commercial: the global AI market is projected to grow from $387.45 B (2022) to $1,394.30 B by 2029 (Fortune Business Insights), and Accenture estimates AI could raise profitability rates by an average of 38% by 2035. AI is becoming indispensable — which is exactly why bias and opacity are business risks, not just moral ones.

O'Neil's claim — the three conditions a model must satisfy

From Weapons of Math Destruction: How Big Data Increases Inequality and Threatens Democracy (Cathy O'Neil), models must be:
  1. Transparent — if the model is not understandable, its application can lead to unintended consequences.
  2. Built on clear, quantifiable objectives.
  3. Equipped with a self-correcting mechanism and a process to be audited regularly, with new inputs and outputs constantly reconsidered.
Managers and data scientists must be aware of the social and long-term effects of mathematical models and algorithms.

Impacts on organizations

  • New organizational units: an analytics department, a BI department, a data science department, an AI department — and new C-suite titles: Chief Data Officer, Chief AI Officer.
  • Transformation & competitive advantage — enhancing digital transformation; e.g. Amazon uses robots to cut cost and dominate online commerce. (Case 11.1: 1-800-Flowers.com uses intelligent systems — SAS Marketing Automation, IBM Watson personalization, SAS Enterprise Miner — for competitive advantage.)

Impact on managers' activities, performance & job satisfaction

  • Less expertise/experience is required to make many decisions; faster decision-making; less reliance on experts and analysts.
  • Power is redistributed among managers — the more information and analysis capability they possess, the more power they have.
  • Support for complex decisions; information for high-level decisions is expedited or even self-generated; routine decisions get automated.

Impacts on jobs and work

General agreement: intelligent systems will create many new jobs (as automation always has), many people will need retraining, and the nature of work will change. No consensus on when, how much, or how to deal with it. A key prediction: AI will accelerate the polarization of the labor market — growth at the top and bottom tiers, losses in the middle.

The pessimistic case — massive job losses

  • They move very fast; they can take a wide variety of jobs, including office-focused, administrative and non-physical ones; their comparative advantage over manual labour is large and growing; they are already taking professional jobs (financial advisors, paralegals, medical specialists); AI capabilities are growing rapidly. In Russia, robots already teach mathematics in schools.
  • Examples: Elon Musk foresees autonomous truck convoys worldwide within 10 years (electric, economical, fewer accidents — but thousands of drivers lose jobs); Amazon Go cashierless stores (3,000 more planned); post offices already delivering with autonomous vehicles. "In short, there is a chance for massive unemployment."
  • Case 11.2 — jobs robots have already taken: online marketers, financial analysts/advisors, anesthesiologists, diagnosticians, surgeons, financial & sports reporters (Sherman 2015); middle management, commodity salespeople, report writers, accountants/bookkeepers (Palmer 2017); cashiers, toll-booth operators, fast-food workers, drivers (McFarland 2017). Low-risk jobs: nurses, doctors, dentists, youth sport coaches, social workers.

The optimistic case — work changes rather than disappears

Low-skill jobs will be taken by machines; high-skill jobs may not. Jobs may be redesigned either to be low-skilled so they can be automated or high-skilled so humans execute them. Manyika (McKinsey): many human activities have automation potential; productivity growth from robotics/AI/ML will be tripled; AI will create many new, high-paying jobs; and since more than half the world is still offline, change won't be too rapid.

Tips for success (managerial)

  • Digital capabilities must come before AI. ML is powerful but not the solution to all problems.
  • Do not put technology teams solely in charge of intelligent technologies — add a business partner.
  • Prioritize a portfolio approach to AI initiatives. The biggest challenges will be people and business processes, not the technology.
  • Almost all businesses that use intelligent systems increase income and profit; top-leadership support is necessary for an AI transformation.

Dealing with the change — 9 societal measures

Use learning and education to facilitate change · involve the private sector in training/retraining · governments incentivize private investment in human capital · build digital infrastructure · develop innovative income and wage schemes · carefully plan the transition and deal properly with displaced employees · handle new technology-enabled technologies · focus on new job creation, particularly digital jobs · capture the productivity increase opportunities.

The data-science profession & the citizen data scientist

  • Citizen science — members of the general public voluntarily help conduct scientific research, at any stage: designing experiments, collecting data, helping analyse, developing reports.
  • Citizen data scientist — a business user who has not been fully trained in the mathematics and statistics of data science, but has learned to use the software tools to build models for their own business use. Enabled by visual, intuitive analytics tools. Why it matters: it helps alleviate the shortage of data-science professionals (the talent gap from the opening vignette).
  • Closing caution: new topics constantly appear in analytics/data science/AI — some are fads, others will make a transformative difference.
