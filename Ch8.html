Key idea

Core message: Prescriptive analytics = making the decision with an analytical model. Descriptive and predictive analytics create the foundation (the choice alternatives); prescriptive analytics picks the best possible decision — by optimization when the problem can be expressed mathematically, and by simulation when it is too complex for a closed-form optimum. Remember the chain: Descriptive, Predictive → Prescriptive.

Example framing from the slides: profit maximisation based on optimal spending on promotions and product/service pricing; or choosing which customers get a promotional offer to maximise response within a budget.

Model-based decision making

Modeling is the key element of prescriptive analytics — mostly mathematical modeling. Real-world successes are published in INFORMS outlets (Interfaces, ORMS Today, Analytics Magazine).

Marquee example: TurboRouter — a DSS for ship routing; within just a few weeks the company saved $1–2M. Same family: the School District of Philadelphia (opening vignette) used prescriptive analytics to find the optimal way to award school-bus route contracts, and ExxonMobil (Case 8.1) optimises ship transport for its downstream business.

Major modeling issues

  • Problem identification & environmental analysis — information collection; understand the setting first.
  • Variable identification — find the variables and their relationships; tools: influence diagrams, cognitive maps.
  • Forecasting (predictive analytics) — feeds the model; more information → better prediction.
  • Multiple models — a decision system may combine several models, each representing a different part of the problem; models can be static (single snapshot) or dynamic (evaluate over time).
  • Model management — models, like data, must be managed to maintain integrity and applicability → model-based management systems (MBMS).
  • Knowledge-based modeling — DSS usually uses quantitative models; expert systems use qualitative, knowledge-based models (bridge to the Expert Systems lecture).
  • Current trends — cloud-based modeling tools; transparent (multidimensional/visual) models; "model of models" (e.g. influence diagrams used to build and solve models).

Exam trap: optimization guarantees the best solution of the modeled problem; simulation and heuristics only find good-enough ones. AHP (used in R17 and Tutorial 2 / SuperDecisions) sits in the "few alternatives" row.

Structure of mathematical models

A quantitative model mathematically links three kinds of variables (plus intermediate results):

  • Decision variables — what the manager controls (what to produce, staffing levels, ad budget).
  • Uncontrollable variables / parameters — the environment (inflation rate, competition, machine capacity, regulations).
  • Result variables — the outcomes (profit, market share, total cost, customer satisfaction).
AreaDecision variablesResult variablesUncontrollable
Financial investmentInvestment alternatives & amountsTotal profit, risk, ROI, EPS, liquidityInflation rate, prime rate, competition
MarketingAdvertising budget, where to advertiseMarket share, customer satisfactionCustomer income, competitors' actions
ManufacturingWhat/how much to produce, inventory, compensationTotal cost, quality, employee satisfactionMachine capacity, technology, material prices
ServicesStaffing levelsCustomer satisfactionDemand for services

Components are linked by algebraic equations or inequalities. Simplest: P = R − C. Present-value example: P = F / (1+i)ⁿ = 100,000 / (1.1)⁵ = 62,092.

ZoneWhat you knowConsequence
CertaintyComplete knowledge — all outcomes knownMay yield the optimal solution
RiskSeveral outcomes per decision, with known probabilitiesProbabilistic decision making — compute expected values
UncertaintySeveral outcomes, probabilities unknownUse optimistic/pessimistic approaches; gather knowledge to move toward risk
Case 8.3 — American Airlines: a "should-cost" model to assess the uncertainty of supplier bids for shipment routes, reducing the risk of over- or under-paying suppliers.

Decision modeling with spreadsheets

The spreadsheet is the most popular end-user modeling tool: flexible, easy, powerful add-in functions, programmable (macros), built-in what-if analysis and goal seeking, simple database management, seamless integration of model and data, supports both static and dynamic models.

  • Static example: simple loan calculation of monthly payments — F = P(1+i)ⁿ and the annuity formula A = P·[i(1+i)ⁿ]/[(1+i)ⁿ−1].
  • Dynamic example: the same loan model extended with the effects of prepayment over time.
Cases: the Pennsylvania Adoption Exchange used a spreadsheet model to better match children with families (Case 8.4); Metro Meals on Wheels Treasure Valley used Excel to find optimal meal-delivery routes (Case 8.5) — proof that "prescriptive analytics" can live in Excel, not just in specialised solvers.

Optimization via mathematical programming

Mathematical programming: a family of tools for problems where a decision maker must allocate scarce resources among competing activities to optimise a measurable goal. The optimal solution = the best possible solution to the modeled problem.

Linear programming (LP): a mathematical model for optimal resource allocation in which all relationships are linear.

LP problem characteristics (5)

  1. Limited quantity of economic resources.
  2. Resources are used to produce products or services.
  3. Two or more ways (solutions, programs) to use the resources.
  4. Each activity yields a return in terms of the goal.
  5. Allocation is restricted by constraints.

LP modeling steps

1 Identify decision variables, objective function, coefficients, constraints→2 Represent the model (LINDO formulation / Excel cells)→3 Run & observe results

Common optimization model families

Assignment (best matching), dynamic programming, goal programming, investment (maximise ROI), linear & integer programming, network models (planning/scheduling), nonlinear programming, replacement (capital budgeting), simple inventory models (EOQ), transportation (minimise shipment cost).

Case 8.6: a mixed-integer programming model schedules physicians at the University of Tennessee Medical Center — integer variables because you can't assign 0.4 of a doctor.

Worked LP example — the MBI product-mix model (know this cold)

Problem: MBI Corporation builds two computers, CC-7 and CC-8. How many of each per month to maximise profit?

CC-7CC-8RelationLimit
Labor (days)300500≤200,000 / month
Materials ($)10,00015,000≤8,000,000 / month
Units CC-71—≥100
Units CC-8—1≥200
Profit ($)8,00012,000Maximise

Algebraic formulation

Decision variables: X₁ = units of CC-7, X₂ = units of CC-8.

Max Z = 8000·X₁ + 12000·X₂

subject to  300X₁ + 500X₂ ≤ 200,000 · 10,000X₁ + 15,000X₂ ≤ 8,000,000 · X₁ ≥ 100 · X₂ ≥ 200

Solution (via Excel Solver): X₁ = 333.33 (slides: 333), X₂ = 200 → total profit ≈ $5,066,667 per month (slides: 5,066.66 in $'000). Interpretation: build as many high-labor-efficiency CC-7s as labor allows after meeting the CC-8 minimum — the labor constraint is binding, materials are not.

Multiple goals, sensitivity analysis, what-if, goal seeking

Multiple goals

Most managerial problems have multiple objectives to attain simultaneously. Handling methods: utility theory, goal programming, expressing goals as constraints in LP, a points system. Difficulties: hard to get a single organisational goal; goal importance changes over time; goals viewed differently across levels; group dynamics; assessing priorities.

The three analysis modes (favourite exam contrast)

ModeQuestion it answersExample
Sensitivity analysisHow does a change in inputs affect outputs?Which variables matter enough to estimate better; can be automatic or trial-and-error
What-if analysisWhat happens to the solution if a variable/assumption changes? (scenario analysis)"What if capacity at the milling station rises 40% — what is the impact on output?"
Goal seekingBackwards: start from the goal, find the input neededBreak-even: "To make profit = 0, how many units must we sell per month?"

Sensitivity analysis also helps eliminate variables, revise models to remove too-large sensitivities, add detail about sensitive variables, and obtain better estimates of them.

Decision analysis with decision tables & decision trees

Decision table: a tabular model — alternatives (decision variables) × states of nature (uncontrollable) → projected results.

The investment example (goal: maximise yield after one year)

AlternativeSolid growth (%)Stagnation (%)Inflation (%)
Bonds12.06.03.0
Stocks15.03.0−2.0
CDs (time deposits)6.56.56.5
  • Under uncertainty: optimistic approach (best of the best → stocks, 15%) vs pessimistic approach (best of the worst → CDs, 6.5%).
  • Under risk (known probabilities: growth 0.5, stagnation 0.3, inflation 0.2) — compute expected values: Bonds = 12(.5)+6(.3)+3(.2) = 8.4% ✅ · Stocks = 15(.5)+3(.3)−2(.2) = 8.0% · CDs = 6.5% → choose bonds by EV.
  • Multiple goals (yield, safety, liquidity): bonds = 8.4 / high / high · stocks = 8.0 / low / high · CDs = 6.5 / very high / high — the "best" now depends on goal weights.

Decision trees: graphical representation of the relationships; can be induced from data (data-mining trees, Ch.5) or driven from experts (knowledge-driven); good for multiple criteria and complex relationships, but cumbersome with many alternatives. Tools: Mind Tools, TreeAge, Palisade.

Simulation

Definition: simulation is the "appearance" of reality — a technique for conducting experiments (what-if analysis) with a computer on a comprehensive model of a system to assess its dynamic behaviour. Used when the system is too complex for other DSS techniques.

Major characteristics

  • Imitates reality (vs a model that merely represents it) — captures richness in shape and behaviour.
  • A technique for experiments; descriptive, not normative — it evaluates what you try, it does not produce the optimum by itself.
  • Rule of thumb: use simulation only when numerical optimization is not possible.

Worked example — the supermarket model

Objective: hire the least-cost combination of checkout clerks & baggers that keeps queue length under a limit 95% of the time.
Decision variables: number of open checkout counters; number of baggers.
State (uncontrollable) variables: number of shoppers in the store / at each open counter.
Data needed: arrival-rate statistics, distribution of shopping times, checkout process statistics.

Advantages vs disadvantages

AdvantagesDisadvantages
Straightforward theory; great time compression; experiment with alternatives; reflects the manager's perspective; handles a wide variety of problems and real complexities; produces performance measures; often the only tool for non-structured problemsCannot guarantee an optimal solution (descriptive model → prescriptive outcomes only indirectly); time-demanding & costly to build; models are problem-specific (solutions don't transfer); so easy to sell that managers may overlook available analytical optima; software may require special skills

Simulation methodology — 7 steps

1 Define problem→2 Construct model→3 Test & validate→4 Design experiments→5 Conduct experiments→6 Evaluate results→7 Implement solution

Simulation types & visual interactive simulation

  • Stochastic vs deterministic — stochastic models have one or more random variables as inputs.
  • Time-dependent vs time-independent.
  • Monte Carlo simulation — X = A + B where A, B, X are all probability distributions; sample repeatedly to build the output distribution.
  • Discrete-event vs continuous vs agent-based simulation.
  • Implementation flavours: visual simulation and/or object-oriented simulation.

Visual Interactive Simulation (VIS) / Visual Interactive Modeling (VIM): uses computer graphics and animation to fix conventional simulation's opacity; often integrated with RFID and GIS; allows interactive/immersive sensitivity analysis, up to virtual reality and immersive presence.

Software: Simio, SAS Simulation (SAS OR), Lumina, Oracle Crystal Ball, Palisade, Rockwell Arena (full survey list at orms-today.org).
