One-sentence takeaway: a systematic review of ≈40 AHP + GIS studies shows AHP is an effective, transparent way to fuse expert judgment with quantitative data for flood/landslide susceptibility mapping (via pairwise comparison, eigenvector weights and a Consistency Ratio < 0.1), but criteria must be context-specific to each hazard.

Main Argument

AHP, a multi-criteria decision analysis (MCDA) method, is an effective and transparent way to combine quantitative data with qualitative expert judgment for disaster site-suitability / susceptibility mapping — but methodological variability across studies threatens the robustness of results.

Problem / Context

Flood and landslide risk mapping involves many criteria of unequal importance. Purely data-driven maps ignore valuable expert knowledge, while purely expert-opinion maps can be inconsistent. AHP addresses this by integrating both, typically paired with GIS for spatial mapping.

Data & Method

The paper is a systematic review of numerous published studies (≈20 landslide + ≈20 flood susceptibility studies) that apply AHP combined with GIS. It explains the AHP mechanics in detail: build a hierarchy (goal → criteria → sub-criteria → alternatives) → construct a pairwise comparison matrix using the Saaty 1–9 scale → derive criteria weights via the matrix’s eigenvector → check the Consistency Ratio (CR < 0.1) to validate the judgments → combine weighted criteria layers through a weighted overlay in GIS to produce the final susceptibility map. The review also compares different sub-criteria weighting schemes (straight, reciprocal, exponential ranking, rank-order centroid).

Key Results

Landslide susceptibility maps are dominated by slope, lithology, and land-use/land-cover; flood susceptibility maps are dominated by rainfall intensity, proximity to rivers, and drainage density. The review concludes AHP is not universally applicable — criteria selection must be context-specific to the hazard — and calls for a standardised methodology to improve transparency and comparability across future AHP disaster-mapping studies.

5 Lessons Learned

  1. AHP corresponds to the “few alternatives” optimization row of Ch.8’s Table 8.1 — it structures a messy multi-criteria decision into a solvable hierarchy.
  2. The Consistency Ratio is what makes an expert’s pairwise judgments defensible: CR < 0.1, otherwise redo the comparisons.
  3. AHP’s strength is fusing hard data with expert opinion; its weakness is subjectivity built into the choice of criteria and weights.
  4. AHP + GIS (weighted overlay of criteria layers) is the standard pairing for spatial disaster-risk decisions.
  5. Different hazards need different criteria — don’t copy a landslide-susceptibility model onto a flood problem, or vice versa.

Exam focus

Exam angle: “Walk through how AHP would be used to build a landslide-susceptibility map.” Answer: build the goal–criteria–alternatives hierarchy (e.g. slope, lithology, land-use) → pairwise-compare criteria on the Saaty 1–9 scale → derive eigenvector weights → check CR < 0.1 → combine weighted layers in a GIS overlay.
