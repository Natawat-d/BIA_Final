One-sentence takeaway: a systematic review of 40 AHP + GIS studies (20 landslide + 20 flood) shows AHP is an effective, transparent way to fuse expert judgment with quantitative data for susceptibility mapping (via pairwise comparison, eigenvector weights and a Consistency Ratio < 0.1), but criteria must be context-specific to each hazard.

Main Argument

AHP, a multi-criteria decision analysis (MCDA) method, is an effective and transparent way to combine quantitative data with qualitative expert judgment for disaster site-suitability / susceptibility mapping — but methodological variability across studies threatens the robustness of results.

Problem / Context

Site-suitability analysis for disaster-prone areas involves many criteria of unequal importance — environmental (slope, soil, land cover), socio-economic (population, proximity to services), and regulatory (zoning). Purely data-driven maps ignore valuable local expert knowledge, while purely expert-opinion maps can be inconsistent. Introduced by Thomas Saaty in 1980, AHP addresses this by structuring the decision into a hierarchy and integrating both data and expert judgment, typically paired with GIS for spatial mapping. But its very flexibility has produced a diversity of methodologies — wide variation in how criteria are selected, weighted and validated — which threatens consistency and comparability. The review’s gap: no standardised, comparative synthesis existed of which criteria matter most for flood vs. landslide mapping and how weighting practices differ.

Key concepts

AHP hierarchy
A multi-level structure: goal → criteria → sub-criteria → suitability level, each level logically linked to the next.
Saaty 1–9 scale
Translates qualitative judgments into numbers: 1 = equal importance … 9 = extreme importance; reciprocals (1/3, 1/7…) for the inverse comparison. Paper recommends 5–9 criteria (a 20-criteria matrix needs 190 comparisons — cognitively unmanageable).
Eigenvector weights
The pairwise-comparison matrix is column-normalised and row-averaged to derive the priority vector (local weights); global weight = sub-criterion weight × parent-criterion weight.
Consistency Ratio (CR)
CR = CI/RI where CI = (λmax − n)/(n − 1) and RI is a size-dependent Random Index. CR < 0.1 = judgments acceptably consistent; otherwise revise the comparisons. (Worked example in the paper: λmax = 5.199, CI = 0.050, RI = 1.12 for n = 5, CR ≈ 0.05.)
Sub-criteria weighting schemes
Straight ranking (w ∝ n+1−i), reciprocal (1/i), exponential ((n+1−i)P), and rank-order centroid (ROC) — quick alternatives to full pairwise comparison when data or time is limited.

Data & Method

The paper is a systematic review comparing 20 landslide-susceptibility + 20 flood-susceptibility studies (published across many global contexts) that apply AHP combined with GIS. It documents the AHP workflow step by step and then tabulates the criteria each study prioritised, synthesising the most frequently-used factors per hazard type.

  1. Criteria selection — guided by relevance, completeness, independence/non-redundancy, measurability, mutual exclusivity, and stakeholder/expert involvement (Delphi, focus groups, interviews).
  2. Hierarchical structuring — goal → criteria → sub-criteria → suitability level.
  3. Pairwise comparison — build the reciprocal matrix on the Saaty 1–9 scale.
  4. Weight derivation — column-normalise → row-average → eigenvector (priority) weights.
  5. Consistency check — compute CI, look up RI, verify CR < 0.1; otherwise redo comparisons.
  6. Sub-criteria weighting — compare straight / reciprocal / exponential ranking and ROC.
  7. Final map — weighted linear combination F = Σ wi si Ci applied as a weighted overlay in GIS.

Key Results

The synthesis shows clear, hazard-specific criteria dominance — the same factors recur across studies of each hazard, confirming that criteria selection is not arbitrary but is hazard-driven.

HazardMost-prioritised criteria (with frequency across 20 studies)
LandslideSlope (in 16/20; ranked #1 in 12), lithology (18/20), land-use/land-cover (LULC) (15/20); plus aspect (16), curvature (15), elevation (12), distance to rivers/drainage (14), distance to roads (13), rainfall (8, but a critical trigger in tropical regions)
FloodRainfall intensity/precipitation (frequently ranked #1–2), proximity to rivers / drainage density, TWI & flow accumulation, elevation & slope, and LULC (dominant in urban studies)

Regional variation is real: tropical regions (Vietnam, Thailand) emphasise rainfall and the Topographic Wetness Index; mountainous regions (Himalayas, Alps) prioritise seismicity and relative relief; urban settings weight roads and soil cohesion. The review concludes AHP is not universally applicable — criteria must be context-specific — and calls for a standardised methodology to improve transparency and comparability.

5 Lessons Learned

  1. AHP corresponds to the “few alternatives” optimization row of Ch.8’s Table 8.1 — it structures a messy multi-criteria decision into a solvable hierarchy.
  2. The Consistency Ratio is what makes an expert’s pairwise judgments defensible: CR < 0.1, otherwise redo the comparisons.
  3. AHP’s strength is fusing hard data with expert opinion; its weakness is subjectivity built into the choice of criteria and weights.
  4. AHP + GIS (weighted overlay of criteria layers) is the standard pairing for spatial disaster-risk decisions.
  5. Different hazards need different criteria — don’t copy a landslide-susceptibility model onto a flood problem, or vice versa.

Limitations & critique

  • It is a review, not a new method: the paper synthesises existing studies rather than validating a fresh model against ground-truth landslide/flood events.
  • Subjective, expert-dependent weights: pairwise comparisons carry inherent subjectivity and potential bias; the CR check controls consistency but not correctness of the underlying judgments.
  • Methodological variability: because criteria selection and weighting differ so widely across studies, results are hard to compare or reproduce — the paper’s own central critique.
  • Scaling problem: pairwise comparison doesn’t scale — many criteria mean an explosion of comparisons (20 criteria → 190), overwhelming human judgment.
  • Static framework: traditional AHP doesn’t account for temporal change (evolving climate/land-use), and depends on the quality/availability of input data — a real constraint in data-scarce regions. The paper recommends sensitivity analysis, fuzzy-AHP, and hybrid AHP + machine-learning as fixes.

Exam focus

Exam angle: “Walk through how AHP would be used to build a landslide-susceptibility map.” Answer: build the goal–criteria–alternatives hierarchy (e.g. slope, lithology, land-use) → pairwise-compare criteria on the Saaty 1–9 scale → derive eigenvector weights → check CR < 0.1 → combine weighted layers in a GIS overlay. Tie this to SuperDecisions/Tutorial 2, quote that slope/lithology/LULC dominate landslide maps while rainfall/rivers/drainage dominate flood maps, and note criteria must be hazard-specific.
