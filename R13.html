Brain Tumor Classification based on Convolutional Neural Networks with an Ensemble Learning Approach through Soft Voting

Kartika Puspita, Ferda Ernawan, Yuris Alkhalifi, Shahreen Kasim, Aldo Erianda · Universitas Nusa Mandiri Jakarta & Universiti Malaysia Pahang · JOIV: Int. J. Informatics Visualization 9(5), Sept 2025, pp. 1964–1970.

One-sentence takeaway: a soft-voting ensemble of four CNNs (VGG16, MobileNet, ResNet50, DenseNet121) classifies brain-tumour MRI scans into four classes at 97.67% accuracy (Cohen’s Kappa 0.9688) — beating every individual CNN.

Main Argument

No single CNN is best for this problem — a soft-voting ensemble of several CNNs raises brain-tumour classification accuracy above any individual model. Rather than betting on one architecture, the paper shows that averaging the probability outputs of several different, independently-trained CNNs cancels out each model’s individual errors and yields a stronger, more reliable classifier.

Problem / Context

Glioma, meningioma and pituitary tumours are major global health issues. Manual MRI reading by radiologists is slow, labour-intensive and error-prone, especially at scale — creating a clear need for automated, computer-vision-based early detection that can support (not replace) clinical diagnosis.

Data & Method

The dataset is 7,023 MRI images at 512×512 resolution, spanning four classes: glioma, meningioma, pituitary tumour, and no tumour. Four CNN architectures — VGG16, MobileNet, ResNet50, DenseNet121 — are each trained and optimised separately (Adam optimiser, learning rate 0.0001, kernel regulariser 0.001). Their predictions are then combined by soft voting: each CNN outputs a class-probability vector, the four vectors are averaged, and the final class is the argmax of the averaged vector.

Key Results

The soft-voting ensemble reaches 97.67% accuracy with Cohen’s Kappa = 0.9688, outperforming every individual CNN in the ensemble as well as prior published methods on comparable brain-tumour classification tasks.

5 Lessons Learned

  1. CNNs learn image features automatically — no hand-crafted feature engineering is needed; this is Ch.7’s core deep-learning claim in action.
  2. Ensembles beat single models: soft voting averages class probabilities across independently-trained CNNs, cancelling out each model’s individual mistakes.
  3. Transfer learning from pretrained architectures (VGG16, ResNet50, DenseNet121, MobileNet) works well even on a modest, domain-specific medical dataset.
  4. Accuracy alone is not enough in multi-class problems — Cohen’s Kappa corrects for agreement that could occur by chance, giving a more trustworthy performance figure.
  5. Deep learning here is meant to support, not replace, radiologists — it is a screening / decision-support aid layered onto expert judgement.

Exam focus

Exam angle: “Why use an ensemble instead of the single best CNN?” Answer: soft voting averages the four CNNs’ predicted probabilities, which cancels individual model errors and consistently outperforms any one architecture (97.67% accuracy, Kappa 0.9688). Also be ready to explain why Kappa matters alongside accuracy in a 4-class problem.
