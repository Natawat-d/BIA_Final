Expert Systems

Key idea

Core message: An expert system is a computer program that imitates an expert's reasoning process and knowledge to solve problems in a narrow domain. Its defining architecture separates the knowledge (knowledge base of IF–THEN rules) from the reasoning (inference engine) — and it can explain its conclusions. ES do not replace experts; they make scarce expertise widely available so non-experts can work better.

What is an expert system?

  • A computer program that attempts to imitate expert reasoning processes and knowledge in solving specific problems.
  • Historically the most popular applied AI technology — enhances productivity, augments workforces.
  • Works best with narrow problem areas/tasks — depth over breadth.
  • Purpose: transfer expertise from the few to the many — the slides quote a non-expert : expert ratio of 100 : 1.

Important concepts: expert & expertise

  • Expert — a human who has developed a high level of proficiency in making judgments in a specific domain.
  • Expertise — the set of capabilities underlying expert performance:
    • extensive domain knowledge,
    • heuristic rules that simplify and improve problem-solving,
    • meta-knowledge and meta-cognition (knowledge about one's own knowledge),
    • compiled forms of behaviour that afford great economy in skilled performance.
  • Transferring expertise — from expert → computer → non-experts, via four activities: acquisition → representation → inferencing → transfer.
  • Other features: symbolic reasoning (manipulating symbols, not just numbers), degrees/levels of expertise, deep knowledge vs surface knowledge, and self-knowledge (the system can reason about its own reasoning).

Conventional programs vs expert systems

Conventional systemExpert system
Knowledge and control (processing) are mixed together in one programKnowledge base is separate from the processing (inference) mechanism
Doesn't (usually) explain why input data are needed or how results were producedExplanation is a core feature — why a fact is asked, how a conclusion was reached
Needs complete, exact input dataCan operate with incomplete or uncertain information
Changes to the logic are tedious (reprogramming)Changes in the rules are easy to make
Works with numeric data, algorithmic processingWorks with symbolic knowledge, heuristic reasoning
Efficient processing of large databasesEffective handling of large knowledge bases
Execution: step-by-step algorithmExecution: firing whichever rules match the current facts

Architecture — the structure of an expert system

Two environments: the development environment (expert + knowledge engineer build/refine the knowledge base) and the consultation environment (a non-expert user gets advice). Major components:

ComponentRole
Knowledge acquisition subsystemGets knowledge from the human expert(s) & other sources — via the knowledge engineer (knowledge elicitation)
Knowledge baseLong-term memory: stores facts + knowledge rules about the domain
Inference engineThe "brain": chains and fires rules against known facts to derive conclusions
User interfaceQuestion/answer dialogue with the user
Blackboard (workplace)Working / short-term memory: the current problem's facts and intermediate results
Explanation subsystem (justifier)Explains why a fact is requested and how a conclusion was reached
Knowledge-refining systemLearns from experience: analyses performance and refines the rules
Data flow to remember (the slides' architecture figure): Human expert(s) + other knowledge sources → knowledge elicitation → knowledge engineer → knowledge rules → knowledge base. User → questions/answers → user interface → facts → blackboard; inference engine reads facts + rules, fires rules, writes conclusions back to the blackboard; the explanation facility justifies; knowledge refinement sends refined rules back to the base. External data can arrive via the Web.

Knowledge engineering (KE)

Definition: a set of intensive activities encompassing the acquisition of knowledge from human experts (and other sources) and converting it into a repository — the knowledge base. Primary goals: help experts articulate how they do what they do and document that knowledge in a reusable form. (Narrow KE = building the ES; broad KE = the whole knowledge-systems discipline.)

The KE process — 5 stages + feedback loop

1 Knowledge acquisition→2 Knowledge representation→3 Knowledge validation→4 Inferencing (reasoning)→5 Explanation & justification

Raw knowledge → codified knowledge → validated knowledge → solutions, with a feedback loop of corrections and refinements running back through every stage, plus meta-knowledge feeding the inferencing/explanation stages.

Difficulties in KE

  • Experts often cannot articulate how they decide (compiled, tacit knowledge).
  • Experts may be unavailable, expensive, or disagree with each other.
  • Knowledge extraction is slow; testing and refining rules is laborious; domains drift over time.

Validation vs verification (definitions the slides stress)

  • Evaluation — the broad concept: assess the ES's overall value.
  • Validation — deals with the performance of the system (are we building the right system? does it match the expert?).
  • Verification — building the system right: substantiating that it correctly implements its specifications.

Knowledge representation — production rules

Expert knowledge must be represented in a computer-understandable format, organised in the knowledge base. The most common/popular representation: production rules — condition–action pairs: IF … THEN … (ELSE …).

Forms of production rules

  • IF premise, THEN conclusion: "IF your income is high, THEN your chance of being audited by the IRS is high."
  • Conclusion, IF premise: "Your chance of being audited is high, IF your income is high."
  • With ELSE: "IF your income is high OR your deductions are unusual, THEN your chance of being audited is high, ELSE your chance is low."

Knowledge rules vs inference rules (a classic exam contrast)

Knowledge (declarative) rulesInference (procedural) rules
What they stateAll the facts and relationships about the problemHow to solve the problem given certain facts — including meta-rules (rules about rules)
Where storedKnowledge baseBecome part of the inference engine
Example"IF income is high THEN audit chance is high""IF needed data is not known THEN ask the user"; "IF more than one rule applies THEN fire the highest-priority rule first"

Inferencing — forward vs backward chaining

Inference = the process of chaining multiple rules together based on available data. A rule is FIRED when all of its "if" parts (hypotheses) are satisfied; the engine keeps checking rules forward or backward until no more rules can fire or a goal is achieved.

Forward chainingBackward chaining
DirectionData-driven: start from known factsGoal-driven: start from the conclusion to prove
MechanismIf a rule's premises match the situation, assert its conclusion; repeat as new facts appearBegin with a rule's action clause and work backward through the chain looking for a verifiable set of condition clauses
Typical useMonitoring, planning, configuration — "what follows from these facts?"Diagnosis — "could disease X explain these symptoms?"
Mnemonic: forward = facts → conclusions; backward = goal → needed facts. MYCIN-style diagnosis is the standard backward-chaining example; inventory monitoring (below) runs forward.

Explanation — the metaknowledge feature

Human experts justify their advice — so should an ES. Explanation = the ES's attempt to clarify its reasoning, recommendations, or actions (including why it asks a question). The component is the explanation facility, a.k.a. the justifier.

  • Why explanations — "why is this fact being requested?"
  • How explanations — "how was this conclusion reached?" Simple systems explain only the final conclusion; complex ones show the whole chain of fired rules.
  • Purposes: make the system intelligible; uncover shortcomings of the knowledge base; explain unanticipated situations; satisfy users' psychological/social needs; support training and evaluation.

Good ES problems are narrow, judgment-heavy, rule-expressible, have available experts, and reward making expertise available at scale.

Developing an expert system

  1. Define the nature and scope of the problem (narrow it!).
  2. Identify proper experts.
  3. Acquire knowledge — the knowledge engineer elicits and structures it.
  4. Select the building tools — an ES shell (prebuilt inference engine + empty knowledge base, e.g. Exsys) versus complete custom development.
  5. Code the system (write the rules).
  6. Evaluate and launch — validate against the expert before deployment.
Why shells matter: a shell gives you the inference engine, UI, and explanation facility for free — the project reduces to knowledge engineering. Exsys is the slides' example of a popular shell.

Worked rule examples (straight from the slides)

Retail: inventory replenishment

A grocery store's ES decides when and how to reorder stock:

RULE 1: IF current_stock < reorder_point
        AND supplier_lead_time ≤ 3 days
        AND season = "peak"
        THEN action = "URGENT: Order 150% of standard quantity."

RULE 2: IF current_stock < reorder_point
        AND supplier_lead_time ≤ 3 days
        AND season = "off"
        THEN action = "Order standard quantity."

RULE 3: IF current_stock < reorder_point
        AND supplier_lead_time > 7 days
        THEN action = "Order expedited shipping with double quantity."

RULE 4: IF days_until_expiration < 5
        AND product_type = "dairy"
        THEN action = "Apply 30% discount immediately."

Note the ES signature: rules combine multiple conditions (stock level, lead time, season, expiry) into expert-style actions, and each firing can be explained by quoting the rule.

Exercise the lecture poses: an ES for loan approval — a model sketch

How to answer: ① Scope: consumer-loan approve/reject/refer, one product line. ② Experts & acquisition: senior credit officers; elicit their decision criteria (income, credit score, debt-to-income ratio, employment stability, collateral). ③ Representation — production rules, e.g. IF credit_score ≥ 700 AND DTI < 40% THEN decision = approve; IF credit_score < 550 THEN decision = reject; IF 550 ≤ credit_score < 700 AND collateral = yes THEN decision = refer_to_officer. ④ Inference: backward chaining from the goal "approve?" asking the user only for facts still needed. ⑤ Explanation: the justifier cites the fired rules — essential for fair-lending accountability. ⑥ Validate against past officer decisions before launch; refine rules on disagreement.
