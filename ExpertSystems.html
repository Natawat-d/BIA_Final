Expert Systems

Key idea

Core message: An expert system is a computer program that imitates an expert's reasoning process and knowledge to solve problems in a narrow domain. Its defining architecture separates the knowledge (knowledge base of IF–THEN rules) from the reasoning (inference engine) — and it can explain its conclusions. ES do not replace experts; they make scarce expertise widely available so non-experts can work better.

What is an expert system?

  • A computer program that attempts to imitate expert reasoning processes and knowledge in solving specific problems.
  • Historically the most popular applied AI technology — enhances productivity, augments workforces.
  • Works best with narrow problem areas/tasks — depth over breadth.
  • Purpose: transfer expertise from the few to the many — the slides quote a non-expert : expert ratio of 100 : 1.

Important concepts: expert & expertise

  • Expert — a human who has developed a high level of proficiency in making judgments in a specific domain.
  • Expertise — the set of capabilities underlying expert performance:
    • extensive domain knowledge,
    • heuristic rules that simplify and improve problem-solving,
    • meta-knowledge and meta-cognition (knowledge about one's own knowledge),
    • compiled forms of behaviour that afford great economy in skilled performance.
  • Transferring expertise — from expert → computer → non-experts, via four activities: acquisition → representation → inferencing → transfer.
  • Other features: symbolic reasoning (manipulating symbols, not just numbers), degrees/levels of expertise, deep knowledge vs surface knowledge, and self-knowledge (the system can reason about its own reasoning).

Conventional programs vs expert systems

Conventional systemExpert system
Knowledge and control (processing) are mixed together in one programKnowledge base is separate from the processing (inference) mechanism
Doesn't (usually) explain why input data are needed or how results were producedExplanation is a core feature — why a fact is asked, how a conclusion was reached
Needs complete, exact input dataCan operate with incomplete or uncertain information
Changes to the logic are tedious (reprogramming)Changes in the rules are easy to make
Works with numeric data, algorithmic processingWorks with symbolic knowledge, heuristic reasoning

Architecture — the structure of an expert system

Two environments: the development environment (expert + knowledge engineer build/refine the knowledge base) and the consultation environment (a non-expert user gets advice). Major components:

ComponentRole
Knowledge acquisition subsystemGets knowledge from the human expert(s) & other sources — via the knowledge engineer (knowledge elicitation)
Knowledge baseLong-term memory: stores facts + knowledge rules about the domain
Inference engineThe "brain": chains and fires rules against known facts to derive conclusions
User interfaceQuestion/answer dialogue with the user
Blackboard (workplace)Working / short-term memory: the current problem's facts and intermediate results
Explanation subsystem (justifier)Explains why a fact is requested and how a conclusion was reached
Knowledge-refining systemLearns from experience: analyses performance and refines the rules
Data flow to remember (the slides' architecture figure): Human expert(s) + other knowledge sources → knowledge elicitation → knowledge engineer → knowledge rules → knowledge base. User → questions/answers → user interface → facts → blackboard; inference engine reads facts + rules, fires rules, writes conclusions back to the blackboard; the explanation facility justifies; knowledge refinement sends refined rules back to the base. External data can arrive via the Web.

Knowledge engineering (KE) — the process

Definition: a set of intensive activities encompassing the acquisition of knowledge from human experts (and other sources) and converting it into a repository — the knowledge base. Primary goals: help experts articulate how they do what they do, and document that knowledge in a reusable form. (Narrow KE = building the ES; broad KE = the whole knowledge-systems discipline.)

Two things everyone forgets: ① a feedback loop of corrections and refinements runs back through every stage — KE is iterative, not a one-way pipeline; ② meta-knowledge (knowledge about the knowledge) feeds the inferencing and explanation stages — it is what lets the system reason about its own reasoning. Note also the hand-off in the middle: stages 1–3 are done by humans at build time (development environment); stages 4–5 are done by the machine at run time (consultation environment).

Stage 1 — Knowledge acquisition

What it is: extracting knowledge from its sources and transferring it toward the computer. The knowledge engineer interviews and observes the domain expert, a process called knowledge elicitation. Sources are not only people: documented sources count too — books, manuals, regulations, case files, databases, sensors, the Web.

The bottleneck. Acquisition is the hardest and slowest stage of the whole project — the classic "knowledge-acquisition bottleneck." The reason is compiled knowledge (from the definition of expertise): experts have automated their skill to the point that they can no longer articulate how they decide. Ask a doctor why they suspected bipolar rather than depression and you get "experience" — not a rule.

Elicitation techniques

TechniqueHow it worksGood for
Structured interviewPre-planned questions walking the expert through the domainBroad coverage; the usual starting point
Unstructured interviewOpen conversation, expert leadsEarly scoping; discovering what you don't know to ask
Protocol analysis (think-aloud)The expert solves a real case while narrating their reasoning; the session is recorded and codedGetting at tacit reasoning the expert can't state abstractly
ObservationWatch the expert work, unpromptedRevealing what experts do vs what they say they do
Case / repertory-grid analysisCompare past cases to surface the attributes the expert discriminates onTurning judgment into explicit criteria
Rule induction / machine learningDerive rules automatically from historical dataWhen data is plentiful but experts are scarce — the data-driven alternative

Difficulties (the classic exam list)

  • Experts cannot articulate how they decide (tacit/compiled knowledge) — the core problem.
  • Experts may be unavailable, expensive, or unwilling (why would they automate away their own scarcity?).
  • Multiple experts disagree — whose rules win? (R18 notes that rules and their coefficients vary by expert.)
  • Knowledge is domain-specific and drifts over time; extraction is slow and testing is laborious.
  • The knowledge engineer may misunderstand the domain and mis-encode what they heard.
Shallow vs deep knowledge: shallow = surface heuristics ("if the engine won't start and the lights are dim, the battery is flat") — easy to elicit, brittle at the edges. Deep = understanding the underlying causal model (how a battery, alternator and starter actually interact) — far harder to acquire, but degrades gracefully on novel cases.

Stage 2 — Knowledge representation

What it is: organising the raw knowledge into a computer-understandable format and storing it properly in the knowledge base. Raw knowledge in → codified knowledge out. This is where an interview transcript becomes something an inference engine can actually fire.

Representation schemes

SchemeIdeaNote
Production rulesCondition–action pairs: IF … THEN … (ELSE …)The most common and most popular — and what "rule-based ES" means. See the production-rules section.
Semantic networksA graph of concepts (nodes) joined by relationships (links), e.g. is-a, has-aGood at inheritance and association
FramesAn object with named slots and values (a template per concept)Object-like; groups everything known about one entity
Decision tables / decision treesTabular or branching enumeration of conditions → actionsAlso Ch.8's "few alternatives" tools — easy for experts to check
Predicate logicFormal logic statements with quantifiersPrecise, provable; less intuitive for domain experts
OntologiesA formal specification of concepts and relations in the domainThe modern form — R18 encodes psychiatrists' rules as SWRL rules over an ontology

Why representation choice matters

  • It determines what the inference engine can do — rules chain, frames inherit, networks traverse.
  • It determines whether the expert can still read and correct their own knowledge (a rule they can check; a weight matrix they cannot — this is the whole ES-vs-neural-network trade-off).
  • Recall the ES signature: knowledge is kept separate from the reasoning mechanism. Representation is what makes that separation possible — you can change the rules without reprogramming the engine.

Stage 3 — Knowledge validation

What it is: testing the codified knowledge until you trust it. Codified knowledge in → validated knowledge out. Typically done by running test cases — cases with known correct outcomes — and comparing the system's conclusions against the expert's conclusions, refining the rules on every disagreement.

The three terms the slides insist on — do not mix them up:
  • Evaluation — the broad concept. Objective: assess the ES's overall value (is it worth having at all?).
  • Validation — the part of evaluation dealing with the performance of the system. "Are we building the right system?" Does it reach the same conclusions an expert would?
  • Verification — "building the system right": substantiating that the system is correctly implemented to its specifications. Does it do what the spec said, without internal defects?
Mnemonic: validation = right system (external, vs the expert) · verification = system right (internal, vs the spec).

What verification actually hunts for — rule-base anomalies

AnomalyMeaning
Conflicting rulesSame conditions, contradictory conclusions
Redundant rulesTwo rules say the same thing — harmless but bloats the base
Subsumed rulesOne rule is a more specific case already covered by another
Circular rulesA chains to B chains back to A → the engine loops forever
Unreachable / dead-end rulesA rule whose conditions can never be satisfied, or whose conclusion is never used
Missing rules (gaps)A legitimate combination of facts that fires nothing — the system goes silent
R18 is the model answer here: the rule base was validated by rigorous testing with expert psychiatrists and against 120 clinical samples, reaching a 93.33% success rate on only 17 symptoms — plus a user-acceptance score of 3.56/4. Note that they measured both technical performance and human acceptance: an ES no clinician trusts is a failed ES, however accurate.

Stage 4 — Inferencing (reasoning)

What it is: the point where the machine takes over. The inference engine takes the validated knowledge base plus the facts of the current case (from the user, via the blackboard/working memory) and chains rules together to derive a conclusion. Everything before this stage was preparation; this is the ES actually thinking.

The match–fire cycle

Match
find rules whose IF-parts are satisfied by current facts→Resolve conflict
several rules eligible? pick one (meta-rules, priority)→Fire
execute the THEN-part, assert the new fact→Repeat
until no rule can fire, or the goal is reached

A rule is FIRED when all of its hypotheses (the "if parts") are satisfied. The engine checks every rule in the knowledge base — forward or backward — looking for rules it can fire, and continues until no more rules can fire or a goal is achieved.

The two directions (detail in the chaining section)

  • Forward chaining — data-driven: start from known facts, assert conclusions, repeat. "What follows from this?"
  • Backward chaining — goal-driven: start from a hypothesis, work backward hunting for facts that would verify it. "What would I need to believe this?" — and it only asks the user for facts it still needs, which is why diagnostic systems feel like an efficient interrogation.

Conflict resolution — when several rules could fire

This is what inference rules (meta-rules) are for; they live in the engine, not the knowledge base: IF more than one rule applies THEN fire the one with the highest priority value first; IF needed data is not known THEN ask the user. Other common strategies: fire the most specific rule, the most recently triggered, or simply the first in order.

Reasoning under uncertainty

Real expertise is rarely black-and-white, so an ES must cope with incomplete or uncertain information (one of its advantages over conventional programs). Mechanisms: certainty factors (MYCIN's approach — attach a confidence to each rule and conclusion), Bayesian probability, and fuzzy logic. R18's rules likewise carry coefficients that vary by expert, and it evaluates combinations of symptoms rather than single facts.

Stage 5 — Explanation & justification

What it is: the ES defends its answer. Human experts justify and explain their actions — so should an ES. The component is the explanation facility, a.k.a. the justifier. Formally, explanation is "an attempt by an ES to clarify its reasoning, recommendations, or other actions (including asking a question)." It draws on meta-knowledge — the system's knowledge about its own knowledge — which is why the slides call explanation a metaknowledge feature. The output of this stage is the solution, delivered with its reasoning attached.

The two explanation types (both examinable, know the difference)

WHY explanationHOW explanation
User asks"Why are you asking me this?""How did you reach that conclusion?"
Asked whenDuring the consultation, when the system requests a factAfter a conclusion or recommendation is given
System answers byShowing the rule it is currently trying to fire — i.e. what the fact is needed forShowing the chain of rules that fired to get there
Depth—Simple systems explain only the final conclusion; most complex systems provide the whole chain of rules used

Why explanation is essential (5 purposes)

  1. Make the system more intelligible to the user.
  2. Uncover shortcomings of the knowledge base — a nonsensical explanation exposes a bad rule (so explanation is a debugging tool, feeding the KE feedback loop).
  3. Explain unanticipated situations.
  4. Satisfy users' psychological and social needs — people don't act on advice they can't interrogate.
  5. Support training and evaluation — a novice learns the domain by reading the system's reasoning.
Why this is the stage that matters commercially: explanation is exactly what a neural network cannot give you (Ch.7's black-box problem). It is why R18's rule-based diagnosis earned a 3.56/4 acceptance score from psychiatrists where a black-box classifier would have been rejected, and it is why a loan-approval ES can survive a fair-lending audit — it can cite the rules it fired. Accuracy gets you a model; explanation gets you a system people will actually use.

Knowledge representation — production rules

Expert knowledge must be represented in a computer-understandable format, organised in the knowledge base. The most common/popular representation: production rules — condition–action pairs: IF … THEN … (ELSE …).

Forms of production rules

  • IF premise, THEN conclusion: "IF your income is high, THEN your chance of being audited by the IRS is high."
  • Conclusion, IF premise: "Your chance of being audited is high, IF your income is high."
  • With ELSE: "IF your income is high OR your deductions are unusual, THEN your chance of being audited is high, ELSE your chance is low."

Knowledge rules vs inference rules (a classic exam contrast)

Knowledge (declarative) rulesInference (procedural) rules
What they stateAll the facts and relationships about the problemHow to solve the problem given certain facts — including meta-rules (rules about rules)
Where storedKnowledge baseBecome part of the inference engine
Example"IF income is high THEN audit chance is high""IF needed data is not known THEN ask the user"; "IF more than one rule applies THEN fire the highest-priority rule first"

Inferencing — forward vs backward chaining

Inference = the process of chaining multiple rules together based on available data. A rule is FIRED when all of its "if" parts (hypotheses) are satisfied; the engine keeps checking rules forward or backward until no more rules can fire or a goal is achieved.

Forward chainingBackward chaining
DirectionData-driven: start from known factsGoal-driven: start from the conclusion to prove
MechanismIf a rule's premises match the situation, assert its conclusion; repeat as new facts appearBegin with a rule's action clause and work backward through the chain looking for a verifiable set of condition clauses
Typical useMonitoring, planning, configuration — "what follows from these facts?"Diagnosis — "could disease X explain these symptoms?"
Mnemonic: forward = facts → conclusions; backward = goal → needed facts. MYCIN-style diagnosis is the standard backward-chaining example; inventory monitoring (below) runs forward.

Explanation — the metaknowledge feature

Human experts justify their advice — so should an ES. Explanation = the ES's attempt to clarify its reasoning, recommendations, or actions (including why it asks a question). The component is the explanation facility, a.k.a. the justifier.

  • Why explanations — "why is this fact being requested?"
  • How explanations — "how was this conclusion reached?" Simple systems explain only the final conclusion; complex ones show the whole chain of fired rules.
  • Purposes: make the system intelligible; uncover shortcomings of the knowledge base; explain unanticipated situations; satisfy users' psychological/social needs; support training and evaluation.

Problem areas suitable for expert systems

The classic ten generic categories — recognise the task type from a scenario:

CategoryTask
InterpretationInfer situation descriptions from observations/sensor data
PredictionInfer likely consequences of situations
DiagnosisInfer malfunctions/diseases from symptoms
RepairPrescribe and execute remedies for malfunctions
DesignConfigure objects under constraints (e.g. XCON)
PlanningDevelop sequences of actions to reach goals
MonitoringCompare observations to expected outcomes, flag exceptions
DebuggingPrescribe remedies for faults in a design/program
InstructionDiagnose and correct student performance (tutoring)
ControlGovern overall system behaviour (interpret–predict–monitor–act)

Good ES problems are narrow, judgment-heavy, rule-expressible, have available experts, and reward making expertise available at scale.

Developing an expert system

  1. Define the nature and scope of the problem (narrow it!).
  2. Identify proper experts.
  3. Acquire knowledge — the knowledge engineer elicits and structures it.
  4. Select the building tools — an ES shell (prebuilt inference engine + empty knowledge base, e.g. Exsys) versus complete custom development.
  5. Code the system (write the rules).
  6. Evaluate and launch — validate against the expert before deployment.
Why shells matter: a shell gives you the inference engine, UI, and explanation facility for free — the project reduces to knowledge engineering. Exsys is the slides' example of a popular shell.

Worked rule examples (straight from the slides)

Retail: inventory replenishment

A grocery store's ES decides when and how to reorder stock:

RULE 1: IF current_stock < reorder_point
        AND supplier_lead_time ≤ 3 days
        AND season = "peak"
        THEN action = "URGENT: Order 150% of standard quantity."

RULE 2: IF current_stock < reorder_point
        AND supplier_lead_time ≤ 3 days
        AND season = "off"
        THEN action = "Order standard quantity."

RULE 3: IF current_stock < reorder_point
        AND supplier_lead_time > 7 days
        THEN action = "Order expedited shipping with double quantity."

RULE 4: IF days_until_expiration < 5
        AND product_type = "dairy"
        THEN action = "Apply 30% discount immediately."

Note the ES signature: rules combine multiple conditions (stock level, lead time, season, expiry) into expert-style actions, and each firing can be explained by quoting the rule.

Exercise the lecture poses: an ES for loan approval — a model sketch

How to answer: ① Scope: consumer-loan approve/reject/refer, one product line. ② Experts & acquisition: senior credit officers; elicit their decision criteria (income, credit score, debt-to-income ratio, employment stability, collateral). ③ Representation — production rules, e.g. IF credit_score ≥ 700 AND DTI < 40% THEN decision = approve; IF credit_score < 550 THEN decision = reject; IF 550 ≤ credit_score < 700 AND collateral = yes THEN decision = refer_to_officer. ④ Inference: backward chaining from the goal "approve?" asking the user only for facts still needed. ⑤ Explanation: the justifier cites the fired rules — essential for fair-lending accountability. ⑥ Validate against past officer decisions before launch; refine rules on disagreement.
