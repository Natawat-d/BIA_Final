One-sentence takeaway: in-depth interviews at 20 organisations across three sectors show that AI-enhanced BI shifts analytics from reactive to proactive/augmented — automated insight generation, natural-language querying and prescriptive recommendation — but adoption is gated by data quality, skills, trust and governance.

Main Argument

AI + BI shifts analytics from reactive to proactive / augmented — systems autonomously detect patterns and suggest optimal strategies, moving organisations away from intuition-driven decisions and static, backward-looking BI reports.

Problem / Context

Organisations face an overwhelming volume of data and rapidly-evolving market dynamics, yet managers still often decide from intuition and rear-view BI reports — consistent with the classic rational, bounded-rational and intuitive decision models. BI is evolving through generations (BI 1.0 reporting → BI 2.0 interactive → BI 3.0/4.0 AI-augmented), mirroring Davenport’s Analytics 1.0–4.0 (traditional BI → big data → digital transformation → AI-driven augmented analytics). Although AI-in-BI’s potential is widely acknowledged, existing studies focus on technological architectures or theoretical models, leaving a gap: little empirical, qualitative evidence on how organisations actually implement AI-enhanced BI and how managers perceive the benefits, barriers and synergy in real decision-making.

Key concepts

Augmented analytics
AI (ML + NLP) autonomously generates insights, detects anomalies and gives real-time recommendations — the “system proposes, manager disposes” model that defines BI 4.0 / Analytics 4.0.
Reactive → proactive shift
From reporting “what happened” (historical, descriptive) to automatically suggesting “what to do next” (predictive + prescriptive).
BI as a paradigm
The paper frames BI not as a mere tool but as a comprehensive information-management paradigm spanning collection, processing, analysis, visualisation and decision support.
Natural-language querying / NLP
Lets managers interact with BI in plain language (chatbots, semantic search), reducing the need for advanced analytical expertise.
Hyperautomation
Combining AI + Robotic Process Automation (RPA) + BI to fully automate decision-making processes — one of the areas that benefits most from AI–BI integration.
In-Depth Interview (IDI)
A qualitative method giving rich, context-sensitive insight into managers’ lived experience — chosen because it suits exploring an emerging, complex phenomenon.

Data & Method

A qualitative study using in-depth interviews (IDIs), conducted November–December 2024 across 20 organisations in three economic sectors — services (IT, finance, logistics, consulting), trade (retail, wholesale, e-commerce), and manufacturing (production, energy, automotive). Selection criteria: minimum 50 employees, at least 6 months of AI+BI use, active use of both in decision-making. Respondents were managers, data analysts, and AI/BI specialists. Interview responses were analysed in Power BI using two text-analysis techniques — Word Cloud Analysis (word-frequency) and Treemap Visualization (hierarchical proportions) — and reported as counts of “indications” (how many respondents mentioned each theme).

Key Results

Capabilities identified: automated insight generation, natural-language querying, forecasting, and prescriptive recommendation. The interview counts quantify managers’ priorities and pain points.

The paper frames BI as a comprehensive information-management paradigm, with AI-enhanced BI acting as a decision enabler conditional on organisational readiness — a shift from retrospective reporting to proactive, data-driven decision support.

5 Lessons Learned

  1. BI’s future is augmented analytics: the system proposes candidate strategies, and the manager still disposes (decides).
  2. The core shift is reactive → proactive — from reporting “what happened” to automatically suggesting “what to do next”.
  3. Trust and explainability, not raw model accuracy, are the real gating factor for managerial adoption.
  4. The classic barriers persist across studies: data quality, skills, and governance — the same list that shows up as Big Data’s critical success factors.
  5. BI is fundamentally a management paradigm, not just a technology — AI amplifies it but does not replace organisational judgement.

Limitations & critique

  • Qualitative & small sample: the paper’s own limitation — only 20 organisations in three sectors, so findings have limited generalisability to other industries.
  • Self-report bias: interview responses reflect personal experience and organisational culture, and may over- or under-state real impact.
  • Benefits not quantified: results are counts of mentions, not measured performance gains — no ROI, accuracy or productivity metrics.
  • Context-dependent: the retail sector dominated the reported benefits, suggesting results skew toward fast-moving, data-rich industries.
  • The authors call for mixed-methods / quantitative follow-up, broader sectors, and deeper study of ethics, algorithm transparency and managerial competencies — all beyond this study’s scope.

Exam focus

Exam angle: “How does AI change what BI does for managers?” Answer: it shifts BI from reactive reporting to proactive/augmented analytics — automated insights, natural-language querying, prescriptive suggestions — drawn from 20 real organisations, but adoption still hinges on data quality (the #1 barrier, 20 indications), skills shortage, trust and governance. Frame BI as a management paradigm, not just technology.
