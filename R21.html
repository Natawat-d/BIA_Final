An Integrated Big Data Analytics Architecture for Resilience: A Case Study of Last-Mile Agri-Food Delivery

Morteza Alaeddini & Alireza Asgari · ICN Business School / CEREFIGE, Université de Lorraine, Nancy; Université Grenoble Alpes · Procedia Computer Science 253 (2025) 1621–1630 (ISM 2024).

One-sentence takeaway: a case study at a B2B2C last-mile food-delivery company proposes a layered Big Data Analytics architecture that integrates descriptive + predictive + prescriptive analytics to improve supply-chain resilience — anticipation, response and recovery from disruption — where isolated analytics tools fall short.

Main Argument

Supply-chain resilience requires an integrated Big Data Analytics architecture that combines descriptive, predictive and prescriptive analytics — not a collection of isolated analytics tools bolted together.

Problem / Context

Last-mile delivery (LMD) of agri-food must guarantee uninterrupted access to essential food supplies, yet is highly vulnerable to disruption (weather, traffic, demand shocks). Existing generic Big Data Analytics reference models do not explicitly address resilience as a design goal.

Data & Method

The paper uses a case-study approach at a large B2B2C last-mile food-delivery company, drawing requirements from industry standards, academic research, and the company’s specific needs. It reviews three groups of prior literature — general BDA reference models (e.g. Kaufmann’s big-data management model), resilient BDA systems (BDA + IoT for disaster prediction and monitoring), and domain-specific architectures — and then proposes a layered architecture (ingestion → storage → analytics → decision) that carries all three analytics types end to end.

Key Results

The proposed architecture enhances resilience by integrating descriptive, predictive and prescriptive analytics, enabling improved anticipation, response and recovery from supply-chain disruptions — descriptive analytics shows what happened, predictive analytics anticipates disruption, and prescriptive analytics plans the recovery response.

5 Lessons Learned

  1. Architecture matters: the resilience value comes from integrating the analytics layers together, not from any single model in isolation.
  2. The descriptive → predictive → prescriptive ladder maps directly onto anticipate → respond → recover in a disruption context.
  3. Resilience is a BDA use case in its own right — disruption detection and recovery, not just day-to-day operational efficiency.
  4. Reference architectures must be adapted to the domain (here, agri-food last-mile delivery) — a generic big-data stack is not sufficient on its own.
  5. IoT / sensor streams (the Velocity V of Big Data) are what make real-time disruption anticipation possible in the first place.

Exam focus

Exam angle: “How should a company architect Big Data Analytics for supply-chain resilience?” Answer: a layered architecture (ingestion → storage → analytics → decision) that integrates descriptive, predictive and prescriptive analytics together — mapping onto anticipate / respond / recover — rather than deploying isolated analytics point solutions.
