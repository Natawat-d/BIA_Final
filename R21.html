One-sentence takeaway: a case study at a B2B2C last-mile food-delivery company proposes a 5-layer Big Data Analytics architecture that integrates descriptive + predictive + prescriptive analytics to improve supply-chain resilience — anticipation, response and recovery from disruption — where isolated analytics tools fall short.

Main Argument

Supply-chain resilience requires an integrated Big Data Analytics architecture that combines descriptive, predictive and prescriptive analytics — not a collection of isolated analytics tools bolted together.

Problem / Context

Last-mile delivery (LMD) is the final, customer-facing leg of the agri-food supply chain (AFSC), and must guarantee uninterrupted access to essential food supplies — especially in crises (pandemics, labour shortages, traffic disruption). It faces multiple pressures at once: consumers want faster delivery (38% want same-day or less), demand fluctuates with the shift to e-commerce and organic food, ~30% of the world’s food is wasted, and LMD’s carbon footprint is projected to rise 36% by 2030 with city-centre vehicle bans looming. Big Data Analytics (BDA) is a recognised tool for resilience, but the paper’s literature review finds a clear gap: existing reference models each cover only one or two analytics types and none (except a high-level framework by Keates et al., which lacks a detailed design) comprehensively integrates all three — descriptive, predictive and prescriptive — for LMD resilience.

Key concepts

Supply-chain resilience
The collective ability of AFSC stakeholders to keep food supply stable by anticipating disruptions, delaying their impact, enabling rapid recovery, and learning afterwards — mapped in this paper to anticipate → respond → recover.
Three analytics types
Descriptive = what happened (dashboards, reporting); predictive = what will happen (demand/disruption forecasting); prescriptive = what to do (route/inventory recommendations). Integration of all three is the paper’s core contribution.
B2B2C model
The case company is an intermediary between food suppliers and consumers, delivering groceries via a mobile/web app — a business-to-business-to-consumer platform.
Reference architectures
Standardised BDA blueprints (NIST NBDRA roles/fabrics; ISO/IEC 20547 BDRA layers) that give a common language and layered structure but must be adapted to a domain.
Architectural views
The design is expressed as UML logical (classes/packages), process (ETL + prediction sequence), and implementation (5-layer component) views.

Data & Method

The paper uses a case-study approach (Yin) at a large Middle-East B2B2C last-mile food-delivery company that currently uses only descriptive analytics (data warehouse + Power BI) and is transforming toward predictive and prescriptive analytics. Requirements were drawn from 17 semi-structured interviews with board members, CXO-level managers and consultants, then triangulated against the company’s strategic plan, meeting minutes, BI dashboards and employee feedback. It reviews three literature groups (general BDA reference models e.g. Kaufmann; resilient BDA systems e.g. BDA + IoT for disaster prediction; domain architectures) and two standards (NIST NBDRA, ISO/IEC 20547) before designing the architecture.

  1. 7 strategic drivers identified: supply-chain visibility, demand forecasting, risk management, operational efficiency, customer-centricity, agility, and sustainability — each requiring a mix of descriptive/predictive/prescriptive analytics.
  2. Functional requirements: multi-stakeholder roles, data aggregation from diverse sources (IoT, DBs, web services, sensors), real-time tracking & GPS visibility, dynamic route optimisation, demand forecasting for inventory, customer-behaviour analysis.
  3. Non-functional requirements: scalability, data security/privacy, reliability/availability, usable interfaces, interoperability.
  4. Architecture (5 layers): Big Data Services (governance/quality) → Framework (storage, processing, OLAP) → Provision (ingestion/metadata) → Applications (descriptive/predictive/prescriptive logic) → Consumption (end-user interfaces + feedback).

Key Results

The proposed architecture enhances resilience by integrating descriptive, predictive and prescriptive analytics end-to-end, mapping onto anticipate → respond → recover. It is a design contribution, so “results” are the architecture’s properties and its claimed advantages over prior models rather than performance numbers.

Analytics typeRole in the architectureResilience mapping
DescriptiveData warehouse + dashboards show past operations & consumer behaviourAnticipate (situational awareness)
PredictiveForecast demand, traffic, disruptions from historical + external dataRespond (proactive optimisation)
PrescriptiveRecommend routes, inventory adjustments, adaptive strategiesRecover (optimal action)

Compared with prior reference models, the authors argue their design integrates directly with big-data platforms (lower latency, higher scalability than Park’s IRIS-RM and Keates’ MPSCA), targets supply-chain resilience specifically (unlike energy/manufacturing-focused models), and emphasises real-time decision support and usable visualisation with feedback loops.

5 Lessons Learned

  1. Architecture matters: the resilience value comes from integrating the analytics layers together, not from any single model in isolation.
  2. The descriptive → predictive → prescriptive ladder maps directly onto anticipate → respond → recover in a disruption context.
  3. Resilience is a BDA use case in its own right — disruption detection and recovery, not just day-to-day operational efficiency.
  4. Reference architectures must be adapted to the domain (here, agri-food last-mile delivery) — a generic big-data stack is not sufficient on its own.
  5. IoT / sensor streams (the Velocity V of Big Data) are what make real-time disruption anticipation possible in the first place.

Limitations & critique

  • Conceptual, single-case study: the architecture is proposed from one company’s requirements and has not been tested with real-world data or experiments — the paper’s own stated limitation.
  • No quantitative evaluation: resilience improvements are argued conceptually; no metrics demonstrate the claimed gains over baseline models.
  • Unproven generalisability: requirements come from a single Middle-East B2B2C firm; transferability to other industries/regions needs further empirical study.
  • Interview-based requirements: the 17 interviews carry self-report and selection bias, only partly mitigated by triangulation.
  • Implementation gap: the design stops at architecture views; the actual build, integration and adoption challenges are left to future work.

Exam focus

Exam angle: “How should a company architect Big Data Analytics for supply-chain resilience?” Answer: a layered architecture (5 layers: Services → Framework → Provision → Applications → Consumption) that integrates descriptive, predictive and prescriptive analytics together — mapping onto anticipate / respond / recover — rather than deploying isolated analytics point solutions. Flag that it’s a conceptual single-case design with no quantitative test.
