Main Argument

Invasion control needs an integrated DDSO (data-driven–simulation–optimization) framework, because static strategies fail against a dynamic, spatially spreading invasion managed under a fixed budget.

Problem / Context

Spartina alterniflora is invading China’s coastal wetlands, posing a major ecological and economic threat. Management budgets are limited, and the invasion itself is dynamic, uncertain and spatially heterogeneous — conditions under which fixed, one-off management strategies quickly become outdated.

Data & Method

The framework couples three modules: (1) data-driven ecological parameterization from multi-source observations, producing time-varying parameters that reflect evolving invasion conditions; (2) simulation of the plant’s life-cycle transitions and spatial dispersal; and (3) a mixed-integer optimization module that allocates control budgets and intervention intensities across space and time to generate cost-effective schedules under fixed budget constraints. The framework is benchmarked against plain System Dynamics (SD) and a simpler Simulation–Optimization (SO) approach, with scenario analysis across different budget levels and invasion intensities.

Key Results

DDSO outperforms both SD and SO on budget efficiency, and surfaces two counter-intuitive management insights: (1) effectiveness depends more on having a coordinated optimization framework than on the scale of investment; and (2) economically efficient strategies inherently favour highly uneven spatial resource allocation rather than spreading effort evenly. The results underscore the value of early intervention and cross-regional coordination.

5 Lessons Learned

  1. The full prescriptive stack in one system: descriptive (data) → predictive (simulation) → prescriptive (optimization).
  2. Coordination beats budget — how resources are allocated matters more than how much money is spent.
  3. Optimal spatial allocation is uneven — spreading effort equally across regions is economically inefficient.
  4. Mixed-integer programming is the right tool for combined yes/no-and-how-much decisions across many locations and time periods.
  5. Time-varying, data-driven parameters let the plan adapt; static models mismanage a dynamic system.

Exam focus

Exam angle: “Why integrate data-driven parameterization with simulation and optimization, rather than optimizing alone?” Answer: static optimization can’t track a spreading, budget-constrained invasion; DDSO’s time-varying parameters + simulation + mixed-integer optimization beat SD and SO on budget efficiency, and reveal that uneven, coordinated spatial allocation outperforms even spreading.
