Comparative Analysis of Machine Learning Algorithms for Health Insurance Pricing

Main Argument

Insurance pricing should not be guesswork. The paper argues that ensemble regression (specifically random forest) predicts health-insurance premiums more accurately than linear or ridge regression, giving insurers a data-driven way to keep pricing both profitable and cost-effective for customers.

Problem / Context

Post-COVID risk and market uncertainty rose sharply, and insurers must price health packages within a profitable threshold. Historically, pricing relied heavily on consultants’ experience and intuition rather than systematic modelling — a gap the paper addresses with a rigorous machine-learning comparison.

Data & Method

Working from the US Health Insurance dataset, the authors compare four regression models: multiple linear regression, ridge regression, XGBoost, and random forest. Models are evaluated on four metrics — MAE, MSE, RMSE, R² — so that no single metric can hide a weakness. The best-performing model is then further tuned with GridSearchCV hyperparameter search. Feature-importance charts and actual-vs-predicted plots are used to make the results interpretable for a managerial audience.

Key Results

Random forest wins on all four metrics (R² ≈ 0.85, rising to ≈ 0.86 after tuning). XGBoost beats both linear and ridge regression. Hyperparameter tuning via GridSearchCV improves 3 of the 4 metrics (all except MAE). Overall, ensemble methods clearly outperform linear models on this pricing task.

5 Lessons Learned

  1. Always compare several algorithms rather than assuming one (e.g. linear regression) is adequate for a pricing problem.
  2. Ensemble methods — bagging (random forest) and boosting (XGBoost) — beat linear/ridge regression on non-linear pricing data.
  3. Use multiple evaluation metrics (MAE, MSE, RMSE, R²) since a model can win on one metric and lose on another.
  4. Hyperparameter tuning (GridSearchCV) delivers real but not universal gains — it improved 3 of 4 metrics here, not all four.
  5. Visualisation (feature importance, actual-vs-predicted plots) is what turns a statistical model into a decision managers can act on.

Exam focus

Exam angle: “Which algorithm should an insurer trust for pricing, and how do you know?” Answer: compare several regressors on multiple metrics (MAE/MSE/RMSE/R²) — here random forest wins on all four, XGBoost is second, and tuning buys a further, metric-dependent improvement.
