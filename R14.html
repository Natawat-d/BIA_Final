One-sentence takeaway: comparing four regression algorithms on the US Health Insurance dataset, random forest wins on all four error metrics (R² ≈ 0.85, ≈0.86 after GridSearchCV tuning), confirming that ensemble methods beat linear/ridge regression for pricing.

Main Argument

Insurance pricing should not be guesswork. The paper argues that ensemble regression (specifically random forest) predicts health-insurance premiums more accurately than linear or ridge regression, giving insurers a data-driven way to keep pricing both profitable and cost-effective for customers.

Problem / Context

Post-COVID risk and market uncertainty rose sharply, and insurers must price health packages within a profitable threshold while still competing on price. Historically, pricing relied on consultants’ experience, static generalized linear models (GLM), and intuition rather than dynamic, data-driven modelling — a gap the paper addresses with a rigorous four-algorithm comparison. The authors note that GLM “is neither real-time nor dynamic,” and that prior related work (e.g. an ensemble-of-boosting-and-stacking study) had already shown ensembles beating single learners for medical-cost prediction, but without a clean side-by-side comparison of linear, ridge, XGBoost and random forest on one dataset with four metrics.

Key concepts

Ridge regression
Linear regression with an added penalty term (λ) on coefficient size, which trades a little bias for lower variance — useful when predictors are multicollinear.
XGBoost (gradient boosting)
Trees added sequentially, each correcting the errors of the previous ones (boosting) — contrast with random forest’s parallel, independent trees (bagging).
Random forest
Bagging: many decision trees are trained in parallel on bootstrapped samples/features, then their predictions are averaged — reduces the overfitting of a single decision tree.
GridSearchCV
Exhaustive search over a grid of hyperparameter combinations, each evaluated by cross-validation, to find the setting that generalises best.

Data & Method

Working from the US Health Insurance dataset (age, sex, BMI, number of children, smoking status, region → charges), the pipeline runs: load data (Pandas) → exploratory data analysis (distribution of charges is right-skewed; age, smoking status and BMI visibly relate to charges) → pre-processing (no missing values; LabelEncoder converts the three categorical columns — sex, smoker, region — to numeric) → 70/30 train-test split → fit four regression models.

Key Results

Random forest wins on all four metrics before tuning; XGBoost is a clear second, ahead of both linear models. After GridSearchCV tuning, random forest improves on 3 of 4 metrics (MSE, RMSE, R²) but MAE ticks up slightly.

5 Lessons Learned

  1. Always compare several algorithms rather than assuming one (e.g. linear regression) is adequate for a pricing problem.
  2. Ensemble methods — bagging (random forest) and boosting (XGBoost) — beat linear/ridge regression on non-linear pricing data.
  3. Use multiple evaluation metrics (MAE, MSE, RMSE, R²) since a model can win on one metric and lose on another.
  4. Hyperparameter tuning (GridSearchCV) delivers real but not universal gains — it improved 3 of 4 metrics here, not all four.
  5. Visualisation (feature importance, actual-vs-predicted plots) is what turns a statistical model into a decision managers can act on.

Limitations & critique

  • Single dataset, few features: only six predictors from one US dataset — won’t automatically generalise to other markets, regulatory regimes or richer feature sets (claims history, medical codes).
  • Interpretability trade-off: random forest is harder to explain to a regulator or customer than a linear/ridge coefficient table, even though it predicts better — a real tension for justifying an individual premium.
  • Tuning gave mixed results: GridSearchCV improved 3 of 4 metrics but made MAE slightly worse, showing tuning is not a free win on every metric.
  • No external validation: the model is evaluated on a single train/test split of one dataset, not a hold-out year or an independent population.
  • The paper itself flags deep learning (CNN/RNN) and hybrid ensembles as unexplored future directions that might push accuracy further.

Exam focus

Exam angle: “Which algorithm should an insurer trust for pricing, and how do you know?” Answer: compare several regressors on multiple metrics (MAE/MSE/RMSE/R²) — here random forest wins on all four (R² 0.85→0.86 after tuning), XGBoost is second, and linear/ridge lag behind on non-linear pricing data. Be ready to note the interpretability-vs-accuracy trade-off as a critique.
