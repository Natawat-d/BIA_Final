Expert Systems
One-sentence takeaway: a rule-based expert system built from an ontology and SWRL rules — encoding psychiatrists’ heuristics about 17 symptoms — classifies MDD vs. manic/depressive Bipolar Disorder at a 93.33% success rate, with a high clinician-acceptance score of 3.56/4.

Main Argument

A rule-based expert system encoding psychiatrists’ heuristic knowledge can distinguish overlapping mood disorders early — a setting where data-hungry machine learning struggles and where misdiagnosis is clinically costly.

Problem / Context

Major Depressive Disorder (MDD) and Bipolar Disorder (BD) share overlapping symptoms (sadness, exhaustion, sleep disorder), so BD is routinely misdiagnosed as MDD — a serious error, because the wrong treatment (e.g. an antidepressant) can actually trigger a manic episode in a misdiagnosed bipolar patient. Roughly 8–12% of the world’s population suffers from one of these disorders, so a reliable early-diagnosis decision-support system could affect community mental health broadly. The paper notes that most ML approaches to mood-disorder diagnosis rely on EEG/sMRI (recorded physiological signals) rather than behavioural symptoms, and that behavioural datasets are scarce — while clinicians favour expert systems precisely because their reasoning is traceable and interpretable, unlike black-box ML. Prior work rarely differentiated all of Mania BD, Depressive BD, MDD and normal individuals in one system, or published its rules/data — the gap this paper fills.

Key concepts

Rule-based expert system
Four components: knowledge base (rules), inference engine (applies rules to facts), explanation facility (traces reasoning), and GUI. Here the DB/GUI/explanation parts are optional add-ons, not core to the ES.
Certainty Factor (CF)
A number expressing degree of belief/disbelief in a hypothesis; each symptom updates the CF of each disorder class, and the class with the highest CF is the prediction. Chosen over fuzzy logic for being more interpretable, better aligned with clinicians’ confidence-based reasoning, and computationally lighter.
Ontology model
Conceptualises the classes (disorders) and their symptoms as objects/attributes, with increasing/decreasing relations between each symptom and each disorder’s CF — making the knowledge shareable, reusable and extensible.
SWRL (Semantic Web Rule Language)
IF-THEN rules compatible with OWL ontology syntax, e.g. Patient(?x) ∧ MoodSwing(?x,True) ∧ SuicidalThoughts(?x,True) → UpdateMBD(?x, FactorC).
Forward chaining
Reasons from the observed symptoms toward a conclusion — chosen over backward chaining, which risked fixing on an assumed diagnosis before enough evidence (e.g. calling every depressive-BD case MDD) was gathered.

Data & Method

The system is built on an ontology plus SWRL semantic rules, developed through two rounds of expert-psychiatrist interviews (first to collect symptom knowledge, second to tune the CF coefficients until the expert was satisfied). Implemented in Prolog + C# with a simple GUI (a 2–3 minute questionnaire). The 17 behavioural symptoms were chosen with clinicians to minimise inputs while still distinguishing the disorders (age/gender were deliberately excluded as too error-prone to weight). There are two rule groups: one evaluates single symptoms, the other combinations of symptoms (e.g. sadness + mood swings together give stronger BD evidence than either alone).

  1. Classes — four: MDD, Mania BD, Depressive BD, and ordinary individuals with mild symptoms.
  2. Symptoms (17) — Sadness, Exhaustness, Euphoric, Sleep disorder, Mood swings, Suicidal thoughts, Anorexia, Anxiety, Try-explaining, Nervous breakdown, Ignore & Move-on, Admitting mistakes, Overthinking, Aggressive response, Optimism, Sexual activity, Concentration.
  3. Dataset — 120 labelled samples (30 per class) from a private clinic, published on Harvard Dataverse; used only for testing, not for tuning the CFs.
  4. Inference — forward-chaining; each symptom updates each class’s CF; highest-CF class wins; a “full explanation” button shows the CF for every class.
  5. Evaluation — confusion matrix (per-class TP/FP/FN/TN) vs. expert labels, plus a 17-question user-satisfaction survey (~76 volunteers).

Key Results

The overall success rate is 93.33% using only the 17 symptoms, with strong per-class metrics from the confusion matrix (Table 7). A separate performance test on limited clinical samples reported 92.45% (49/53); the mispredictions were cases where competing CFs were close or the patient showed a very broad symptom mix. User acceptance averaged 3.56/4 overall (and 3.60/4 on the four GUI-specific questions), reflecting trust in the transparent reasoning.

5 Lessons Learned

  1. Expert systems suit narrow, judgment-heavy domains where expertise is scarce and rules of thumb can be explicitly articulated.
  2. Explainability wins clinical trust — a black-box ML classifier would likely not be accepted for a diagnosis this sensitive.
  3. Rules can capture combinations of symptoms, not just single facts — that combinatorial reasoning is exactly where expert judgment lives.
  4. The system is designed to augment, not replace the psychiatrist — it supports screening and decision-making, but the final call stays human.
  5. Knowledge validation with real experts and real clinical cases is the essential last step of knowledge engineering, not an afterthought.

Limitations & critique

  • Rules must be hand-encoded: the paper’s own stated limitation — a medical expert is required for any change to the rules, and the rules/CF coefficients differ from one expert to another (subjective).
  • Small, narrow evaluation: only 17 symptoms, 120 samples, and 2 disorders (plus normal) — the 17-symptom choice is acknowledged as potentially controversial since every psychiatrist has a unique approach.
  • No learning from data: static rules don’t improve automatically as new cases arrive; they need manual expert upkeep, unlike ML models.
  • Crisp margins: the model uses crisp thresholds; the authors suggest fuzzy variables would be more realistic, and propose coupling with biomarkers via a voting mechanism.
  • Limited scope of disorders: only mood disorders are covered; extending to schizophrenia, OCD, etc. is future work, and no large-scale prospective clinical trial is reported.

Exam focus

Exam angle: “Why choose a rule-based expert system over machine learning for this diagnostic task?” Answer: MDD/BD behavioural data is scarce and the domain is judgment-heavy; a Certainty-Factor rule-based ES encoding psychiatrists’ single-symptom and symptom-combination rules (via ontology + SWRL, forward chaining) reaches 93.33% success with full explainability (WHY/HOW), which earns clinician trust (acceptance 3.56/4).
