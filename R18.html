Expert Systems
One-sentence takeaway: a rule-based expert system built from an ontology and SWRL rules — encoding psychiatrists’ heuristics about 17 symptoms — classifies MDD vs. manic/depressive Bipolar Disorder at a 93.33% success rate, with a high clinician-acceptance score of 3.56/4.

Main Argument

A rule-based expert system encoding psychiatrists’ heuristic knowledge can distinguish overlapping mood disorders early — a setting where data-hungry machine learning struggles and where misdiagnosis is clinically costly.

Problem / Context

Major Depressive Disorder (MDD) and Bipolar Disorder (BD) share overlapping symptoms (sadness, exhaustion), so BD is routinely misdiagnosed as MDD. The wrong treatment can actually trigger a manic episode in a misdiagnosed bipolar patient, making early, explainable diagnosis a genuine community-health need.

Data & Method

The system is built on an ontology plus SWRL semantic rules, developed on the recommendation of expert psychiatrists. There are two rule groups: one evaluates single symptoms, the other evaluates combinations of symptoms (e.g. sadness + mood swings together provide stronger evidence of BD than either alone). The system distinguishes four classes: MDD, manic BD, depressive BD, and ordinary individuals with mild symptoms. It was validated by expert psychiatrists and tested against 120 clinical samples, using only 17 specified symptoms as inputs.

Key Results

The system classifies mental-disorder cases with a success rate of 93.33% using only the 17 symptoms, and achieves a user-acceptance score of 3.56 / 4 — a high score reflecting that clinicians trust the system because its reasoning is transparent and explainable.

5 Lessons Learned

  1. Expert systems suit narrow, judgment-heavy domains where expertise is scarce and rules of thumb can be explicitly articulated.
  2. Explainability wins clinical trust — a black-box ML classifier would likely not be accepted for a diagnosis this sensitive.
  3. Rules can capture combinations of symptoms, not just single facts — that combinatorial reasoning is exactly where expert judgment lives.
  4. The system is designed to augment, not replace the psychiatrist — it supports screening and decision-making, but the final call stays human.
  5. Knowledge validation with real experts and real clinical cases is the essential last step of knowledge engineering, not an afterthought.

Exam focus

Exam angle: “Why choose a rule-based expert system over machine learning for this diagnostic task?” Answer: MDD/BD data is scarce and the domain is judgment-heavy; a rule-based ES encoding psychiatrists’ single-symptom and symptom-combination rules reaches 93.33% success with full explainability (WHY/HOW), which is what earns clinician trust (acceptance 3.56/4).
