One-sentence takeaway: a PRISMA-style systematic review of two decades of research — screening ≈23,000 hits down to studies of actual BI use — codes 53 factors into 10 categories, showing that BI performance depends on effective use, not mere adoption, and that the antecedents are socio-technical (technology, data, organization, leadership, HR, and the individual user).

Main Argument

Research obsesses over BI adoption, but performance actually comes from effective USE — and the antecedents of effective use are technological, organizational, environmental, and human. As the paper puts it, BI “is not only about novel technology and advanced analysis techniques but also about humans, the user who might or might not take advantage of it.”

Problem / Context

Studies of the effective use of BI are limited despite its direct linkage to organisational performance. Most of the field measures intention-to-adopt rather than actual, ongoing use — leaving a gap between what predicts adoption and what actually predicts performance.

Data & Method

The paper conducts a systematic literature review (PRISMA-style) covering more than two decades of research. A very large initial hit set (≈23,000 records) is screened down to quantitative studies specifically examining the antecedents of actual BI use (not adoption intention). An iterative coding process then produces 53 factors, which are synthesised into 10 categories.

Key Results

The 10 categories are: BI system capabilities, technology, data-related factors, organizational factors, HR management, leadership, environmental factors, individual behavioral factors, and individual capabilities (plus infrastructure). Related concepts introduced: BI infusion and the embeddedness of use; effective use itself is measured by the intensity and extent of use.

5 Lessons Learned

  1. Adoption ≠ effective use — buying and installing BI doesn’t mean anyone uses it well; only genuine use drives performance.
  2. The antecedents are socio-technical: system and data quality matter, but so do leadership, HR management, and individual user capability.
  3. Leadership and culture recur as decisive factors across the review — echoing both R2 and the Big Data critical success factors.
  4. Effective use is measurable: via the intensity and extent of use, infusion, and embeddedness in daily work.
  5. Researchers and managers alike should shift focus from acquiring BI to embedding it into everyday decision-making.

Exam focus

Exam angle: “What determines whether BI actually improves performance?” Answer: not adoption but effective use — a systematic review of ≈23,000 studies distills 53 factors into 10 socio-technical categories (system, technology, data, organization, HR, leadership, environment, individual behaviour & capability), reinforcing R2’s point that BI performance is a human, not just technical, outcome.
