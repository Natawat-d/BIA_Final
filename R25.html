One-sentence takeaway: a PRISMA-style systematic review of two decades of research — screening 23,289 hits down to 36 studies of actual BI use — codes 53 factors into 10 categories, showing that BI performance depends on effective use, not mere adoption, and that the antecedents are socio-technical (technology, data, organization, leadership, HR, and the individual user).

Main Argument

Research obsesses over BI adoption, but performance actually comes from effective USE — and the antecedents of effective use are technological, organizational, environmental, and human. As the paper puts it, BI “is not only about novel technology and advanced analysis techniques but also about humans, the user who might or might not take advantage of it.”

Problem / Context

The value of an information-systems investment lies not in its acquisition cost but in its effective use — and in BI the use process is the critical link between the BI asset and organisational performance. Yet Gartner (2018) found 87% of organisations exhibit low BI maturity/utilisation, and studies of the effective use of BI are limited despite its direct linkage to performance. Most of the field measures intention to adopt or the decision to adopt rather than actual, ongoing use, and prior systematic reviews either don’t clearly separate intention/adoption/actual-use or focus on a single BI type (mobile BI, self-service BI) or context (health sector). The knowledge on actual BI use is therefore scattered — the gap this review closes by synthesising the antecedents of the actual use of BI.

Key concepts

Effective use
Burton-Jones & Grange’s definition: employing BI to support goal attainment — here operationalised as the intensity and extent of use, not mere adoption.
Adoption vs. use
DeLone & McLean’s IS-success model: intention to use is an attitude that precedes the behaviour (use); net benefits depend on the degree of actual use — so studying adoption intention isn’t studying use.
BI infusion / embeddedness
Infusion = users adapt their way of working around BI and innovate its use; embeddedness of use = how deeply BI is assimilated into the decision-making process — both are measures of effective use.
PRISMA
Preferred Reporting Items for Systematic Reviews and Meta-Analyses — the standardised identify → screen → eligibility → include protocol used for study selection.
Direction of effect
Each factor is coded as having a positive, no-significant, or negative effect on BI use (e.g. system quality positive; complexity negative).

Data & Method

The paper conducts a PRISMA systematic literature review following Ghapanchi’s four-stage approach (find resources → select studies → synthesise data → present results), covering January 2000–August 2023 across three databases (Business Source Complete, Scopus, ProQuest Central). Three keyword categories (BI concepts AND implementation AND critical-success-factors/antecedents) were combined with Boolean operators.

  1. Identification — 23,289 records retrieved.
  2. Screening — title/abstract screen → 25 (Business Source Complete) + 114 (Scopus) + 17 (ProQuest) = 156 candidates.
  3. Eligibility — 140 available full-text; excluded conceptual/review/qualitative-only papers, algorithm-development papers, adoption-intention studies, and studies without statistical evidence of an antecedent’s significance/direction.
  4. Included — 36 quantitative studies of actual BI use.
  5. Coding — 73 raw factors identified → iteratively merged (2nd & 3rd researcher review) to 53 factors → grouped into 10 categories with direction of effect coded per factor.

Key Results

The 53 factors group into 10 categories across three domains — technology/BI-application, the individual, and the organization.

Related concepts surfaced: BI infusion and the embeddedness of use; effective use is measured by the intensity and extent of use. The review highlights strategy alignment, analytic culture, leadership and individual behaviour as areas needing further examination — reinforcing that BI is a socio-technical, not purely technical, phenomenon.

5 Lessons Learned

  1. Adoption ≠ effective use — buying and installing BI doesn’t mean anyone uses it well; only genuine use drives performance.
  2. The antecedents are socio-technical: system and data quality matter, but so do leadership, HR management, and individual user capability.
  3. Leadership and culture recur as decisive factors across the review — echoing both R2 and the Big Data critical success factors.
  4. Effective use is measurable: via the intensity and extent of use, infusion, and embeddedness in daily work.
  5. Researchers and managers alike should shift focus from acquiring BI to embedding it into everyday decision-making.

Limitations & critique

  • Secondary review, no primary data: it synthesises 36 prior studies rather than collecting new evidence — findings are only as good as the underlying literature.
  • Heterogeneous studies + publication bias: the 36 studies use different models, contexts and unit-of-analysis, and the review inherits any publication bias in what got published.
  • “Effective use” measured inconsistently: studies define/measure use differently (intensity, extent, infusion, satisfaction), so the merged factor set blends non-identical constructs.
  • Correlational, not causal: the antecedent–use associations are statistical, not proof of causation, and directions of effect sometimes conflict across studies.
  • Scope constraints: only English, peer-reviewed, quantitative journal articles from three databases — qualitative insights and grey literature are excluded by design.

Exam focus

Exam angle: “What determines whether BI actually improves performance?” Answer: not adoption but effective use — a PRISMA review screening 23,289 records to 36 studies distills 53 factors into 10 socio-technical categories (BI features, system capabilities, data, technology, organizational, HR, leadership, environmental, individual behaviour & capability), reinforcing R2’s point that BI performance is a human, not just technical, outcome. Cite Gartner’s 87% low-utilisation stat and the adoption ≠ use distinction.
